How many sheets of drywall for a 12 × 14 ft room with a 9-foot ceiling?

600 sq ft net area. 17 sheets of 4×9 (best fit), 19 of 4×8, 13 of 4×12. Why 9 ft ceilings change the math, plus screws, tape, mud, and the hang sequence that kills horizontal seams.

Short answer: a 12′ × 14′ room with a 9′ ceiling needs ~600 sq ft of drywallonce you net the door and one window. That's 19 sheets of 4′×8′ (32 sq ft each), 17 sheets of 4′×9′ (36 sq ft each), or 13 sheets of 4′×12′ (48 sq ft each). Order one extra sheet past the calc, plus 1 box of screws (5 lb), 1 box of mud (3.5 gal), and 250 ft of paper tape. Below is the math, the sheet-size decision, and the spec for a 9′ ceiling specifically — which is where most online calculators get the answer wrong.

Note
9′ ceilings change the math because standard 4′×8′ sheets hung horizontally don't cover the wall in two passes — you need three pieces, which adds a third butt joint per stud bay and a lot of mud. The right answer is almost always 4′×9′ or 4′×12′ sheets, hung vertically.

The formula

Wall area      = perimeter × ceiling height
Ceiling area   = length × width
Total area     = wall area + ceiling area
Net area       = Total area − openings
Sheets         = Net area / sq ft per sheet, round UP, +1 spare

Worked example: 12′ × 14′ × 9′

Walls:
  Perimeter = 2(12 + 14) = 52 ft
  Wall area = 52 × 9     = 468 sq ft

Ceiling:
  Ceiling area = 12 × 14 = 168 sq ft

Gross area:
  468 + 168              = 636 sq ft

Subtract openings:
  Standard door (3' × 7') = 21 sq ft
  Standard window (3'×5') = 15 sq ft
  Total openings           = 36 sq ft

Net area:
  636 − 36               = 600 sq ft

Sheet count by size:
  4'×8'  (32 sq ft):    600 / 32 = 18.75 → 19 sheets + 1 spare = 20
  4'×9'  (36 sq ft):    600 / 36 = 16.67 → 17 sheets + 1 spare = 18
  4'×10' (40 sq ft):    600 / 40 = 15.00 → 15 sheets + 1 spare = 16
  4'×12' (48 sq ft):    600 / 48 = 12.50 → 13 sheets + 1 spare = 14

Why 4′×9′ or 4′×12′ for a 9′ ceiling

Drywall hung horizontally (long edge perpendicular to studs) is the standard install in most US framing — it puts the seam in the middle of the wall, perpendicular to gravity, where it's easiest to tape and float. With an 8′ wall, two 4′×8′ sheets stack perfectly: bottom sheet covers 0–4′, top sheet covers 4–8′.

At a 9′ wall, two 4′×8′ sheets only cover the first 8′. You need a 12′×1′ rip on top of every wall — meaning two extra horizontal seams per wall, multiplied by four walls = eight extra seams, each ~12′ long. That's 96 linear feet of extra seam to tape and mud, just because you used the wrong sheet size.

With 4′×9′ sheets hung vertically, each sheet covers floor to ceiling in one piece. Zero horizontal seams. The 12′ sheet does the same thing more efficiently — a 12′ horizontal sheet on a 14′ wall covers most of it in one piece with a single 2′ vertical butt at the end of the wall.

Note
4′×9′ drywall is widely stocked at HD/Lowe's in residential markets. 4′×12′ is also available but is heavy — an unwieldy 80–100 lb per sheet. If you're working alone or up a ladder, 4′×9′ is the practical choice for a 9′ ceiling.

Sheet thickness for a 12 × 14 room

  • 1/4" sheet — only for skim-coating curved walls or layering over existing drywall.
  • 3/8" sheet — only for double-layer or curved-wall applications.
  • 1/2" standard — the default for residential walls and ceilings — this article's spec.
  • 1/2" lightweight (USG Sheetrock UltraLight) — same coverage, ~25% less weight. Worth the few extra cents.
  • 5/8" Type X — required by IRC R302.6 for garage ceilings under living space, attached-garage common walls, and party walls. Fire-rated.
  • 1/2" or 5/8" Type C — high-rise or commercial fire-rated assemblies. Probably not what a 12×14 residential room needs.

Screws, mud, and tape (worked)

Screws (1¼" coarse for 1/2" drywall on wood):
  Spacing: 12" o.c. on edges, 16" o.c. in field (Code minimum)
  Practical install: ~1 screw per square foot
  600 sq ft × 1 = ~600 screws
  At ~250 screws / lb (1¼" coarse): 600 / 250 = 2.4 lb
  Order: 5 lb box (you'll use it on the next project too)

Joint compound (mud):
  Coverage: ~0.053 lb per sq ft of board (3 coats including taping)
  600 × 0.053 = 32 lb of mud
  3.5 gal bucket of pre-mixed all-purpose: ~50 lb usable
  Order: 1 bucket all-purpose (taping/topping)
         + ¼ bag setting-type 90 (joint embedding) - optional

Paper tape (250 ft per roll, ~$5):
  Linear footage of seams (typical):
    Horizontal seams (4'×9' vertical install): ~0
    Vertical seams (every 4'): perimeter / 4
    52 / 4 = 13 vertical seams × 9' = 117 LF
    Plus inside corners: 4 corners × 9' = 36 LF
    Plus ceiling/wall corners: 52 LF
    Total: ~205 LF
  One 250 ft roll = enough.

For 4'×8' horizontal install (NOT recommended at 9' ceiling):
  Add: 52 LF horizontal mid-seam + 52 LF horizontal top-seam = 104 LF more
  Plus rip-piece edges: 52 LF
  Total: ~360 LF - order 2 rolls.

Fasteners by sheet size

  • 4′ × 8′ horizontal — ~36 screws per sheet.
  • 4′ × 9′ vertical — ~38 screws per sheet.
  • 4′ × 12′ horizontal — ~52 screws per sheet.
  • Code minimum: 12" o.c. on edges (perimeter and butt), 16" o.c. in the field on walls. Ceilings get 12" o.c. everywhere.

The mud schedule (3 coats)

Coat 1 - TAPE coat (setting-type 90 or all-purpose):
  Embed paper tape in mud, knife smooth, leave wet.
  Working time: ~90 min for setting-type, indefinite for premix.

Coat 2 - FILL coat (all-purpose):
  Wider (8" knife) feathered out 6–8" past tape.
  Sand minimal between coats - knock down high spots only.

Coat 3 - SKIM coat (topping or all-purpose, thinned):
  Wider still (10–12" knife), feathered 12–14" past edges.
  This is the coat you sand most.

Total per seam: 3 coats, 24 hr dry between (less for setting-type).
Total schedule: tape Day 1, fill Day 2, skim Day 3, sand Day 4, prime Day 5.

9′ ceiling install: the practical sequence

  • Hang ceiling first, perpendicular to joists. (4'×12' sheets if accessible - most efficient.)
  • Hang walls top-down: top sheet first, butt to ceiling, push tight against tape on the ceiling perimeter.
  • Bottom sheet up against top, leaving ~3/8" gap at the floor (covered by baseboard later).
  • If using 4'×9' vertical: each sheet floor-to-ceiling in one piece. No mid-wall horizontal seam.
  • Inside corners: factory edges meet, no tapered overlap. Embed corner with paper tape (or paper-faced metal corner if planning a clean look).
  • Outside corners: paper-faced metal bead, embed with all-purpose, two skim coats.
Warning
Don't use mesh tape on butt joints. Mesh has no "hold" when the mud cracks — paper tape is what keeps the seam from opening up over a season of humidity changes. Mesh is fine for small-patch repair and corner reinforcement only.

The cost ballpark (12 × 14 × 9′)

4'×9' lightweight (UltraLight) install:
  17 sheets @ $13–16 ea       = $220–270
  5 lb screw box               = $12
  3.5 gal all-purpose mud      = $14
  250 ft paper tape            = $5
  Corner bead (4 outside)      = $20
  Total materials              ≈ $270–320

Comparable 4'×8' install (more seams):
  19 sheets @ $11–14 ea        = $210–270
  Same accessories              + extra tape ($5)
  Total                         ≈ $270–320

The cost is roughly the same. The labor delta is the win:
4'×9' saves 3–4 hours of taping and one extra mud day.

Common mistakes

  • Buying 4'×8' sheets for a 9' wall and ripping to fit. Adds ~30 LF of butt joint per wall.
  • Skipping the 1/4" gap at the floor. Drywall wicks moisture from the slab, then bubbles.
  • Using mesh tape on butt joints (cracks within a year). Paper tape only.
  • Sanding too aggressively between coats and exposing tape edges. Light sanding only between fill coats.
  • Not priming before paint. Drywall paper and joint compound have different absorption - without primer, you can see every seam under one coat of finish.
  • Forgetting Type X on a garage-attached common wall (IRC R302.6). Inspector will fail it.
